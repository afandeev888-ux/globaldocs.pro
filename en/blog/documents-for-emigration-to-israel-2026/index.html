Documents for Emigration to Israel: Complete Checklist 2026

May 2, 2026 18 min read

Israel is one of the most popular destinations for emigration from Russia and CIS countries. The Law of Return opens a path to citizenship for Jews and their relatives, while the developed economy attracts specialists from around the world. In this article, we'll break down the complete list of documents for all emigration programs, explain apostille requirements, Hebrew translation, and common mistakes that can delay the process for months.

Emigration Programs to Israel

Israel offers several legal pathways for emigration, each with its own document requirements. Understanding which program applies to your situation is critical for proper document preparation. Let's examine the main options available to citizens of Russia and CIS countries in 2026.

Repatriation under the Law of Return (Aliyah)

The Law of Return is unique legislation granting Israeli citizenship to Jews, their children, grandchildren, and spouses. This is the most common emigration path for those from the former Soviet Union. According to the law, a Jew is someone born to a Jewish mother or who has converted to Judaism. Repatriation rights extend to three generations: the Jew, their children, and grandchildren, as well as spouses of all these categories. The process takes from several months to a year depending on the complexity of proving Jewish ancestry.

Work Visa (B-1)

The B-1 work visa is issued to foreign specialists with an official invitation from an Israeli employer. For highly qualified IT specialists, there's a simplified Innovation Visa program allowing work in Israeli technology companies. The B-1 visa is valid for up to one year with the possibility of extension. After 5 years of legal work under this visa, you can apply for permanent residency. It's important to understand that a work visa is tied to a specific employer, and changing jobs requires new authorization.

Student Visa (A-2)

Israeli universities — Tel Aviv University, Hebrew University of Jerusalem, Technion in Haifa — attract students from around the world. The A-2 student visa is issued for the study period and allows working up to 20 hours per week during studies. After graduation, there's an opportunity to stay and look for work. Admission requires recognition of your high school diploma or degree, as well as a certificate of English or Hebrew proficiency depending on the study program.

Family Reunification

Israeli citizens and permanent residents can petition for reunification with spouses, minor children, and in some cases parents. The process of reuniting with a spouse from a country with which Israel has no diplomatic relations or complicated relations requires additional checks and can take several years. Documents proving kinship and marriage must be legalized and translated into Hebrew.

Fiancé/Fiancée Visa

For those planning to marry an Israeli citizen, there's a special visa. It's issued for 6 months with the possibility of extension. During this period, the couple must get married — either through the Rabbinate (for Jews), through a church (for Christians), or through the consulate of the foreign partner's country. After the wedding, an application for permanent resident status is submitted.

Repatriation (Aliyah): Documents and Procedure

Repatriation under the Law of Return is the most common path to Israeli citizenship. The process consists of several stages: document collection, application to the Israeli consulate, receiving the repatriation visa, and the actual relocation. Each stage requires thorough preparation, and errors in documents can significantly delay the process.

Who Has the Right to Repatriation?

According to the Law of Return, the following have the right to repatriation: Jews (born to a Jewish mother or converted to Judaism), children of Jews (regardless of whether the other parent is Jewish), grandchildren of Jews, spouses of Jews, spouses of children of Jews, spouses of grandchildren of Jews. Grandchildren of Jews receive permanent resident status rather than automatic citizenship, but can apply for citizenship later. Repatriation rights do not extend to great-grandchildren of Jews unless they have another basis.

Documents Proving Jewish Ancestry

The most important part of the dossier is documents proving Jewish ancestry. These can include: birth certificates of parents, grandparents indicating nationality as "Jewish," Soviet questionnaires and house books indicating nationality, archival documents from registry offices, documents from Jewish communities, old Soviet passports indicating nationality. The more documents you provide, the faster the verification will proceed. The consulate accepts both originals and notarized copies.

Consulate Procedure

After collecting documents, you schedule an appointment at the Israeli consulate (in Moscow, St. Petersburg, or other cities depending on your country of residence). The consul verifies documents, conducts an interview, and makes a decision. If the decision is positive, the repatriation visa is issued the same day. With the visa, you can fly to Israel within 6 months. Upon arrival at Ben-Gurion airport, you receive a teudat oleh (repatriant certificate) and teudat zehut (identity card).

Important: If there are discrepancies in names or dates in the documents (for example, a grandmother is recorded as Chaya in one document and Evgenia in another), the consulate may request additional evidence. We recommend bringing documents to uniformity through the registry office in advance or preparing explanatory documents.

Complete Document Checklist

Below is a comprehensive list of documents required for various emigration programs. Depending on the specific program, some items may not be required or additional documents may be needed. We recommend preparing documents with margin — better to have an extra certificate than to face rejection.

Personal Documents

  • International passport — valid for at least 6 months from the intended entry date. Having blank pages is mandatory. If you have previous international passports, bring them too — the consulate may request travel history.
  • Internal passport — for citizens of Russia and other CIS countries. Copy of all filled pages with notarized translation into Hebrew or English.
  • Birth certificate — mandatory with apostille and notarized translation. Required for all emigration programs. If the certificate is of old format or damaged, obtain a duplicate from the registry office.
  • Marriage certificate — with apostille and translation. Critically important for family reunification and benefit calculations. If the marriage was concluded in Israel, a Russian certificate is not required.
  • Divorce certificate — if previously married. With apostille and translation. Important for verifying marital status.
  • Children's birth certificates — with apostille and translation for processing their visas and status. For children over 14, their passports may be required.
  • Criminal record certificate — from the Ministry of Internal Affairs with apostille and translation. Valid for 3 months. Mandatory for work visa and family reunification; for repatriation may not be required, but we recommend having it.

Documents for Repatriation (Aliyah)

  • Parents' birth certificates — where nationality is indicated as "Jewish." If parents were born before 1917 or in regions where nationality was not indicated, archival documents will be required.
  • Grandparents' birth certificates — to prove Jewish ancestry through a grandmother or grandfather. The older the document, the harder it is to obtain — an archive request may be needed.
  • Soviet passports of parents/grandparents — where nationality is indicated. This is one of the most convincing pieces of evidence.
  • House books and apartment cards — Soviet documents indicating the nationality of all family members. Kept in passport office archives.
  • Archival certificates from registry offices — if nationality is not indicated in birth certificates, you can request an archival certificate from the registry office where parents' nationality is recorded.
  • Documents from Jewish communities — certificates of burial in a Jewish cemetery, entries in metric books, bar/bat mitzvah or chuppah documents.
  • Photos of relatives' graves — if there are burials in Jewish cemeteries, photos of tombstones with Jewish names can serve as evidence.

Education and Qualifications

  • University diploma — original with apostille and notarized translation into Hebrew or English. For recognition in Israel, diploma evaluation through the Ministry of Education will be required.
  • Diploma supplement with grades — apostille and translation are mandatory. The supplement indicates hours by subject, which is important for qualification recognition.
  • High school diploma — for recognition of secondary education for university admission. Apostille and translation required.
  • Hebrew proficiency certificates — ulpan exam, Yael test, or Hebrew University certificate. Hebrew level affects the amount of absorption benefits.
  • Professional development certificates — courses, trainings, internships. Can strengthen a work visa application.

Employment Documents

  • Employment book — copy of all pages with notarized translation. Confirms work experience for a work visa.
  • Employment certificates — on company letterhead indicating position, duties, and period of work. Translation into Hebrew or English is mandatory.
  • Contract with Israeli employer — for B-1 work visa. Must be certified by the Israeli Ministry of Industry and Labor.
  • Work permit — issued by the Israeli Ministry of Interior after employer approval.

For Family Reunification

  • Marriage certificate — with apostille and translation. Additionally, wedding photos, correspondence, joint accounts may be requested — to prove the marriage is genuine.
  • Family composition certificate — from the passport office or MFC. Confirms family ties.
  • Documents of relative in Israel — copy of teudat zehut (identity card), proof of income and housing.
  • Evidence of relationship — correspondence, joint photos, tickets from joint trips, money transfers. Required for fiancé/fiancée visa and spouse reunification.

Financial Documents

  • Bank statement — to prove financial solvency. It's recommended to have the equivalent of at least $10,000 per family.
  • Real estate documents — certificate of ownership (if available). Can be considered as proof of financial guarantees.
  • Sponsorship letter — if someone is taking on financial obligations for your support in Israel.

Apostille for Israel

An apostille is an international form of document legalization confirming the authenticity of the signature and seal. Russia and Israel are parties to the 1961 Hague Convention, so documents with apostille are recognized in Israel without additional consular legalization. This significantly simplifies the process compared to countries not party to the convention.

Which Documents Require an Apostille?

An apostille is placed on originals of documents issued by government bodies: birth, marriage, divorce, death certificates, diplomas, criminal record certificates, registry office documents, court decisions. It's important to understand that an apostille confirms the authenticity of the document itself and the official's signature, but not its content. Documents issued by consulates are not apostilled — they already have international status.

Where to Get an Apostille?

In Russia, different agencies issue apostilles depending on the document type. Registry office certificates are apostilled by the main registry office administration of the subject of the Russian Federation. Diplomas and educational documents are certified by the Ministry of Education or Ministry of Science and Higher Education. Criminal record certificates are apostilled by the Ministry of Justice or Prosecutor General's Office. Court decisions — by the Judicial Department administration. Processing time ranges from 5 to 45 working days depending on the agency and region.

Electronic Apostille (e-Apostille)

Since 2024, Russia has been implementing an electronic apostille system. Israel does not yet universally accept e-Apostille — preference is given to the traditional paper format. We recommend using the classic apostille until Israeli authorities officially announce acceptance of the electronic format.

Important: An apostille is placed only on the original document or a notarized copy. First obtain the document, then get the apostille, and only then make a notarized translation. Violating this sequence is a common mistake leading to having to redo everything.

Translation to Hebrew and English

Israeli authorities accept documents in Hebrew and English. All documents in Russian must be translated and certified. The choice of translation language depends on the specific authority and the purpose of using the document.

Which Language to Choose?

For most procedures, Hebrew is preferred: Misrad HaPnim (Ministry of Interior), Bituach Leumi (National Insurance Institute), Rabbinate, health funds. English works for: academic recognition of diplomas, work in international companies, IT sector, some banking procedures. If unsure, translate to Hebrew — it's the universal option for Israel.

Translation Requirements

Translation is performed by a professional translator and certified by a notary. The translation indicates the translator's details, their signature, and the notary's seal. The translation must be accurate — any discrepancies in names, dates, or titles can lead to rejection. In Israel, sworn translators (materge) also work, whose translations are accepted by all authorities without additional certification.

Sworn Translator in Israel

An alternative is translation by a sworn translator (materge) directly in Israel. The list of sworn translators is available on the Israeli Ministry of Justice website. Translation in Israel usually costs more than in the country of origin, but quality is guaranteed and the document is ready for immediate use.

Sequence of Actions

Correct order of document preparation: obtaining the original document → placing the apostille → notarized translation into Hebrew or English. It is categorically forbidden to first translate the document and then place an apostille on the translation — this makes the document invalid for use abroad.

Common Mistakes

Over years of work, we've seen hundreds of rejections and delays due to mistakes that could have been avoided. Here are the most common problems to pay special attention to when preparing documents for Israel.

Mistake 1: Wrong Sequence

The most frequent mistake — first translating the document and then trying to place an apostille on the translation. An apostille is placed only on the original government-issued document. The sequence is always: original → apostille → translation.

Mistake 2: Expired Validity

A criminal record certificate is valid for 3 months from the date of issue. Many prepare it in advance, and by the time of the consulate visit it's already expired. Order this certificate last, when all other documents are ready and the consulate appointment date is set.

Mistake 3: Name Discrepancies

Differences in name spelling in different documents (for example, Rosa vs Raiza, Moishe vs Moshe) raise questions from the consul. If there are discrepancies, prepare explanatory documents in advance or bring documents to uniformity through the registry office.

Mistake 4: Wrong Translation Language

Some Israeli authorities only accept translations into Hebrew, others — into English. Check the requirements of the specific authority. For repatriation, the consulate usually accepts translations into English, but in Israel itself, Hebrew is needed for Misrad HaPnim.

Mistake 5: Missing Originals

The consulate requires original documents or notarized copies. Simple photocopies are not accepted. If the original is lost, obtain a duplicate from the registry office or relevant authority.

Mistake 6: Incomplete Document Package

The consul may refuse to accept documents if the package is incomplete. Prepare all documents in advance and check against the official list on the consulate website. Better to have an extra document than to be refused acceptance.

Official Resources

When preparing documents for emigration to Israel, we recommend using only official sources of information. Below is a list of verified resources that will help you at all stages of the process.

  • Embassy of Israel in Moscow — official website with visa information, appointment booking for repatriation. Current document requirements are published here.
  • Consulate General of Israel in St. Petersburg — serves applicants from the Northwestern region.
  • Nativ (Lishkat HaKesher) — organization dealing with repatriation issues from CIS countries. Information on procedures and appointment booking for consular checks.

Israeli Government Services

  • Misrad HaPnim (Ministry of Interior) — visa processing, statuses, teudat zehut. Appointment booking through online system.
  • Ministry of Aliyah and Integration — benefits for repatriants, ulpans, employment assistance.
  • Misrad HaChinuch (Ministry of Education) — recognition of diplomas and educational documents.
  • Bituach Leumi (National Insurance Institute) — social security, pension issues.

Apostille and Legalization in Russia

  • Gosuslugi (State Services) — ordering criminal record certificates, registry office appointments for certificate duplicates.
  • Ministry of Justice of Russia — information on apostille for documents issued by justice bodies.
  • Ministry of Education of Russia — apostille for school documents and college diplomas.
  • Ministry of Science and Higher Education of Russia — apostille for university diplomas.

Learning Hebrew

  • Jewish Agency (Sokhnut) — information about ulpans and Hebrew courses for repatriants.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the entire repatriation process take?

From starting document collection to receiving a repatriation visa takes on average 2-6 months. Document preparation — 1-3 months, consulate appointment — 2-4 weeks, review — from 1 day to several months depending on case complexity. With the visa, you can fly within 6 months.

Do I need an apostille on my international passport?

No, an apostille is not placed on passports. A passport is an international document and is recognized without additional legalization. If necessary, a notarized copy of the passport with translation is made.

Can I get a translation done in Israel?

Yes, translation can be done by a sworn translator (materge) in Israel. This is even preferable as Israeli authorities trust local translators. However, the apostille must still be placed in the country where the document was issued.

How to prove Jewish ancestry if documents are lost?

If original documents are lost, you can request duplicates from registry offices, archival certificates, extracts from house books. Documents from Jewish communities, photos of graves in Jewish cemeteries, and witness testimony also help. In complex cases, the consulate conducts an in-depth verification.

Is a criminal record certificate required for repatriation?

Formally, a criminal record certificate is not on the mandatory list of documents for repatriation. However, the consul has the right to request it on an individual basis. We recommend having the certificate on hand, especially if there were any past legal issues.

What benefits are repatriants entitled to?

Repatriants receive an "absorption basket" — a one-time benefit for the initial period, rental assistance (for 6-12 months), free Hebrew courses (ulpan), health insurance, tax discounts on housing and car purchases. The amount of benefits depends on marital status and number of children.

Can I work in Israel with a work visa from a different employer?

No, the B-1 work visa is tied to a specific employer. To change jobs, you need to obtain a new permit from the new employer and update the visa. Working without authorization is a violation and can lead to deportation.
