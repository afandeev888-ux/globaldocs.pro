Complete List of Documents for Repatriation to Israel: Certificates, Apostilles, Translations

Repatriation to Israel is a process that requires thorough document preparation. The decision of the consulate to issue a repatriation visa directly depends on the correct registration of ZAGS certificates, the presence of an apostille, and the quality of the translation. Every year, thousands of people face the need to collect a package of documents from Russia while being abroad. Mistakes in registration, missing apostilles, or incorrectly executed translations can lead to rejection or significant delays in the process. In this article, we will examine in detail the complete list of documents necessary for repatriation (aliyah) to Israel, explain which ZAGS certificates are required, how to get an apostille, how to translate and legalize documents from Russia while in another country.

Who Is Eligible for Repatriation to Israel

The right to repatriate to Israel is enshrined in the Law of Return (Chok Ha-Shvut), adopted in 1950 and amended in 1970. According to this law, every Jew has the right to repatriate to Israel and automatically receive Israeli citizenship. Under the Law of Return, a "Jew" is defined as a person born to a Jewish mother or who has converted to Judaism in accordance with Halakha (and does not belong to another religion).

In addition to Jews themselves, the right to repatriation extends to children of Jews, grandchildren of Jews, spouses of Jews, spouses of children of Jews, and spouses of grandchildren of Jews. Thus, even if you are not Jewish according to Halakha but have Jewish roots along one line, you may be eligible for aliyah. It is important to understand that the burden of proving Jewish roots falls entirely on the applicant: it is you who must provide the consulate with documents confirming your right to repatriation.

In practice, "proving Jewishness" means providing a documentary chain linking you to a Jewish ancestor. This can include birth certificates, marriage certificates, archival records, extracts from synagogue books, military IDs indicating nationality, and other documents. The more supporting documents you provide, the higher the chances of a positive decision from the consulate. Insufficient documentation is one of the most common reasons for rejection or delay in processing a repatriation case.

Complete List of Documents for Repatriation

To apply for repatriation to Israel, you need to collect a set of documents that can be divided into several categories. Each document serves its own purpose: some verify identity, others confirm Jewish roots, and others establish the absence of a criminal record. Let us examine the complete list of documents required for applying to the consulate.

  • Foreign passport — a valid international passport of the applicant. It is needed for identity verification and for placing the repatriation visa. If it is about to expire, we recommend renewing it in advance.
  • Birth certificate — your own birth certificate, as well as birth certificates of your parents and grandparents through whom the Jewish line is traced. These documents are the foundation for proving family connections.
  • Parents' marriage certificate — confirms the change of surname and establishes the connection between parents. If the parents were divorced, a divorce certificate will also be required.
  • Grandparents' marriage/divorce certificates — similarly to the parents' documents, these are necessary to reconstruct the complete chain of family connections and confirm surname changes.
  • Death certificate — if a relative along the Jewish line has died, a death certificate is required to explain why their personal documents cannot be provided.
  • Criminal record certificate — required for all applicants over the age of 14. The document must have an apostille and be translated into Hebrew.
  • 3x4 photographs — usually 2-3 photographs are needed for the application form and document processing.
  • Consulate application form — completed for each family member applying for repatriation.

ZAGS certificates are the foundation for proving Jewish roots. It is through birth, marriage, and death certificates that the family chain from the applicant to the Jewish ancestor is traced. Without a complete set of ZAGS certificates, the consulate will most likely deny repatriation or require additional evidence.

In addition to the main list, the consulate may request additional documents: archival certificates from ZAGS about name or nationality changes, relatives' military IDs indicating nationality, extracts from house registers, photographs of gravestones with Jewish symbols, or letters from synagogues. The more complete the document package, the higher the probability of a quick and positive decision.

ZAGS Certificates: Which Ones You Need and How to Obtain Them

ZAGS certificates are the key category of documents for repatriation. They are what allow you to trace the family chain from you to your Jewish ancestor. The Israeli consulate requires certificates not only for the applicant but also for all relatives along the Jewish line. This means you will need documents of people who may have long since passed away or live in another city, or even another country.

Which ZAGS certificates are needed

Birth certificates: you will need your own birth certificate, your parents' birth certificates, and if the relationship is traced through a grandmother or grandfather — their birth certificates as well. The birth certificate lists the parents, which establishes the chain. Special attention should be paid to the "nationality" field — in Soviet certificates, it was often filled in and can serve as evidence of Jewish origin.

Marriage certificates: your parents' marriage certificates are necessary to confirm surname changes. If your mother changed her surname upon marriage, without a marriage certificate it is impossible to prove she is the daughter of your Jewish grandmother. Grandparents' marriage certificates are needed for the same reason — to track surname changes along the entire line.

Death certificates: if a relative along the Jewish line has died, the death certificate explains to the consulate why you cannot provide their personal documents. Additionally, in some cases, the death certificate indicates the nationality of the deceased, which can also serve as evidence.

Why you need relatives' documents, not just your own

Many applicants are surprised to learn they need to provide not only their own documents but also those of their parents, grandparents, and great-grandparents. The reason is simple: the consulate must be convinced of the existence of Jewish roots, and your personal documents (birth certificate, passport) alone do not prove Jewish origin. The chain of documents must be continuous — from you to the Jewish ancestor through each ZAGS certificate confirming the relationship.

How to obtain duplicate ZAGS certificates

If the original certificates are lost, worn out, or held by relatives who cannot send them, you can order duplicates (repeated certificates) from ZAGS authorities. A repeated certificate has the same legal force as the original. Here are the main ways to obtain duplicates:

1Through the Gosuslugi Portal

If you have a confirmed account on the Gosuslugi portal, you can submit an application for a repeated ZAGS certificate online. Select the ZAGS office that registered the act, fill out the form, and pay the state fee (350 rubles). The ready certificate can be collected in person or through an authorized representative. This method is convenient but requires a confirmed account and knowledge of the specific ZAGS office that registered the act.

Timeline: 5-10 business days

Cost: 350 rubles state fee

2Through ZAGS in Person

You can personally visit the ZAGS office at the place of registration of the civil status act with an application for a repeated certificate. You need to bring your passport and a receipt for the state fee payment. If you apply at a ZAGS office other than the one that registered the act, ZAGS will send a request to the appropriate office, which will increase the waiting time.

Timeline: 1-7 business days (at the place of registration) or up to 30 days (interregional request)

Cost: 350 rubles state fee

3Through an Authorized Representative

If you are abroad, the most practical option is to execute a power of attorney for a representative in Russia. The power of attorney can be notarized at a Russian consulate abroad or at a local notary (with subsequent apostille and translation). The representative will contact ZAGS on your behalf and receive the repeated certificate. You can also use a specialized company that will handle the entire process of obtaining duplicates.

Timeline: 10-30 days + shipping time

Cost: 350 rubles state fee + representative's services

Apostille on Documents for Israel

Israel is a member of the 1961 Hague Convention, therefore an apostille is required for the recognition of Russian documents in Israel. An apostille is a special stamp certifying the authenticity of the document, the signature of the official, and the seal of the issuing authority. Without an apostille, your documents will not be accepted by Israeli authorities, including the consulate when reviewing a repatriation application.

Which documents require an apostille

An apostille is required on all documents issued by Russian government authorities that will be presented in Israel. This includes birth certificates, marriage certificates, divorce certificates, death certificates, criminal record certificates, and other official documents. Without an apostille, the document has no legal force abroad. The only exceptions are documents issued by a consulate or embassy that do not need additional certification.

Where the apostille is placed

The apostille is placed by the competent authority depending on the type of document. For ZAGS documents (birth, marriage, divorce, and death certificates), the apostille is placed by the ZAGS authority of the subject of the Russian Federation where the document was issued. For criminal record certificates and other MVD documents — at the Main Information and Analytical Center of the MVD of Russia (GIAC MVD) or the territorial MVD authority. For notarized documents and copies — at the justice authorities of the subject of the Russian Federation. It is important to contact exactly the authority that is authorized to place an apostille on the given type of document.

How to get an apostille from abroad

If you are outside Russia, obtaining an apostille is possible through an authorized representative or a specialized company. You will need to send the original documents to Russia (by courier) or execute a power of attorney for a representative. The representative will contact the competent authority, pay the state fee, and receive the apostilled documents, then send them back to you. Many specialized companies offer a comprehensive service: obtaining repeated certificates, placing apostilles, translation, and delivery.

Important: An apostille is placed only on the original document. You cannot place an apostille on a photocopy or a notarized copy. If you need a notarized copy with an apostille — the apostille is first placed on the original, then a notarized copy is made. The apostille certifies the original, not the copy.

Cost and timeline for obtaining an apostille

The state fee for placing an apostille is 2,500 rubles per document. The standard processing time is 5-7 business days. In some cases, an expedited procedure is available for an additional fee. When using a specialized company, the cost increases by the service fee, but you save time and avoid the need to interact with government authorities yourself.

Apostille on the original vs. on a notarized copy

There are two approaches to apostilling documents. The first is placing the apostille directly on the original document. This is the preferred option for repatriation, as the Israeli consulate usually requires originals with apostilles. The second is placing the apostille on a notarized copy. This is possible if you do not want to take the original document out of the country. However, for repatriation to Israel, an apostille on the original is recommended, as the consulate verifies the authenticity of the original documents.

Translation of Documents into Hebrew

All documents submitted to the Israeli consulate must be translated into Hebrew. Translation is a mandatory stage in preparing documents for repatriation, and the consulate's decision directly depends on its quality. The translator must be professional and knowledgeable about the specifics of legal and official documents.

Who can perform the translation

Translation of documents for repatriation to Israel can be performed by a sworn (certified) translator who has a license to perform translations for submission to Israeli government authorities. A sworn translator in Israel (meturgeman mushlam) has the right to certify the translation with their signature and seal, giving it legal force. The translation can also be done in Russia with subsequent notarization, but Israeli authorities prefer translations done by Israeli sworn translators.

Notarized translation vs. sworn translation

A notarized translation done in Russia is certified by a Russian notary and confirms the accuracy of the translation. However, in Israel, such a translation is not always recognized without additional certification. A sworn translation done by a licensed translator in Israel carries more weight and is accepted by all government authorities without restrictions. For repatriation, it is recommended to use specifically a sworn translation into Hebrew.

Does the translation need an apostille

The translation itself does not need an apostille. The apostille is placed on the original document before translation. The correct order of actions is: first you receive the original document, then place an apostille on it, and only then have it translated. The apostille text must also be translated — this is a mandatory requirement, as the consulate must understand the contents of the entire document, including the apostille stamp.

Order of processing: First the apostille, then the translation. Never translate a document before placing an apostille — otherwise the apostille text will not be included in the translation, and the document will need to be translated again.

Common mistakes in translations

The most common mistakes that lead to document rejection by the consulate include: inaccurate translation of names and surnames (different transliteration in different documents), omission or distortion of important data (dates, act record numbers, names of ZAGS offices), untranslated stamps and seals, including the apostille text, incorrect translation of legal terms, and lack of certification by a sworn translator. Each of these errors can be grounds for rejection, so the translation must be done by a professional with experience working with repatriation documents.

Criminal Record Certificate for Repatriation

A criminal record certificate (certificate of absence of criminal record) is a mandatory document for all repatriation applicants aged 14 and older. This document confirms that the applicant has no unexpunged or outstanding criminal record, is not the subject of criminal prosecution, and has not been convicted by a court. The Israeli consulate strictly requires a criminal record certificate from each country where the applicant has lived for more than 12 months in the last 10 years.

How to obtain the certificate from abroad

If you are outside Russia, you can obtain a criminal record certificate in several ways: through the Gosuslugi portal (electronic or paper version), through the Russian consulate in your country of residence, through an authorized representative in Russia, or through a specialized company. For repatriation purposes, you need a paper certificate with an apostille, so the electronic version through Gosuslugi will not suffice — it will need to be obtained again in paper form.

Apostille on the criminal record certificate

The apostille on a criminal record certificate is placed by the MVD authority that issued the certificate. The state fee for placing an apostille is 2,500 rubles. The processing time is 3-7 business days. The apostille is placed only on the original certificate. When applying through Gosuslugi, you can select the option for a paper certificate with an apostille, but personal collection at the MVD is mandatory, which complicates the process for those abroad.

Validity period of the certificate

The validity period of a criminal record certificate is not established by law, however in practice the Israeli consulate accepts certificates issued no more than 6 months ago. It is recommended to order the certificate no earlier than 3-4 months before the planned submission of documents to the consulate, to allow time for apostille, translation, and delivery.

What to do if you have a past conviction

Having a past conviction is not an automatic ground for denial of repatriation. If the conviction has been expunged or removed, the certificate will contain information about it, but repatriation is still possible. However, an unexpunged conviction can be a serious obstacle. In each case, the consul makes a decision based on all circumstances. If you have a past conviction, it is recommended to consult with a lawyer specializing in repatriation issues before submitting documents.

Special Considerations for Different Situations

The process of collecting documents for repatriation is rarely standard. Each applicant has a unique situation that may require non-standard solutions. Let us examine the most common problems and ways to resolve them.

If original documents are lost

Losing original ZAGS certificates is a common problem, especially when it comes to grandparents' documents. In this case, you need to order repeated certificates from ZAGS authorities at the place of registration of the act. If you do not know exactly which ZAGS office registered the act, you can send requests to several ZAGS offices or use the services of specialized companies that have access to archives and can find records based on minimal data. A repeated certificate has the same legal force as the original and is fully suitable for repatriation.

If names are spelled differently in different documents

Discrepancies in the spelling of names and surnames are one of the most common problems. For example, in a grandmother's birth certificate she is recorded as "Khaya," while in later documents she appears as "Klara." Or a grandfather's surname is written as "Rozenberg" in one document and "Rosenberg" in another. Such discrepancies raise questions at the consulate. The solution: obtain archival certificates from ZAGS confirming that it is the same person, or, in exceptional cases, a court order establishing the fact of identity.

If documents were issued in the USSR

Documents issued in the USSR before 1991 are recognized by Israel provided an apostille is placed by the competent authority of the successor state. ZAGS certificates issued in union republics are apostilled by the ZAGS authorities of the respective independent states. For example, a birth certificate issued in the Ukrainian SSR is apostilled by the ZAGS authorities of Ukraine. If you cannot travel to the country that issued the document, the apostille can be placed through an authorized representative or a specialized company.

If relatives are from different republics

When the Jewish line passes through relatives who lived in different union republics, document collection is complicated by the need to contact ZAGS offices in different countries. For example, a grandmother was born in Belarus, got married in Ukraine, and the grandfather is from Moldova. In this case, documents from three countries will be needed, each of which must be apostilled in the respective state. This significantly increases the timeline and cost of processing, but is not an insurmountable obstacle. Specialized companies can help obtain documents from all CIS countries.

If there is no direct proof of Jewish heritage

In some cases, direct documents confirming Jewish roots have not survived. Then indirect evidence is used: nationality records in military IDs, work books, house registers; photographs of gravestones with Jewish symbolism; synagogue archives; witness testimony. The consulate reviews each such case individually. Sometimes a genetic test (DNA test) may be required, although it is not a standard requirement and is considered only as additional evidence.
