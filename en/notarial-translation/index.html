What Is a Notarial Translation?

A notarial translation is a translation of a document that has been certified by a notary public, giving it legal force. The process works as follows: a professional translator performs the translation and signs it. The notary then certifies the translator's signature, confirming the translator's identity and professional qualifications. The result is a document that carries legal weight and is recognized by government authorities, courts, immigration offices and other official bodies.

Notarial translation is essential when foreign authorities require legally verified translations of your documents. It is the most widely recognized form of certified translation across the world and is accepted in the vast majority of countries for official purposes.

It is important to understand the distinction between three types of certified translation. A regular translation is simply a linguistic conversion without any legal certification — useful for informational purposes but not accepted by most authorities. A notarial (notary-certified) translation is one where the notary certifies the translator's signature — this is the most common and universally recognized form of certified translation. A sworn translator translation is performed by a translator who has been officially appointed by a government authority or court — for example, a Vereidigter Übersetzer in Germany or a traductor jurado in Spain. Some countries accept only sworn translator translations, while others accept notary-certified translations. Global Docs Pro provides both types.

Germany requires translations by a sworn translator (Vereidigter Übersetzer). Spain requires a traductor jurado. France requires a traducteur assermenté. When Do You Need a Notarial Translation?

A notarial translation is required in a wide range of situations whenever a foreign authority needs to read and verify the content of a document issued in another language. Below are the most common scenarios.

Residence permits, green cards, work visas, family reunification, naturalization. Immigration authorities almost always require certified translations of birth certificates, marriage certificates, police clearance certificates and other supporting documents.

🎓

Education

Diplomas, academic transcripts, certificates of advanced training, school leaving certificates. Universities and credential evaluation agencies require certified translations of all educational documents for admission and degree recognition.

💼

Employment & Business

Employment certificates, employment record books, contracts, powers of attorney for business purposes. Foreign employers and business partners often require certified translations of professional documents.

👨‍👩‍👧

Family Matters

Marriage certificates, divorce decrees, child custody orders, adoption papers, consent letters. Cross-border family proceedings require certified translations of all relevant legal documents.

🏥

Medical

Medical certificates, vaccination records, health examination reports, hospital discharge summaries. Medical institutions abroad require certified translations for treatment, insurance claims and visa medical examinations.

🏛️

Legal & Court

Powers of attorney, court decisions, contracts, inheritance documents, notarized agreements. Courts and legal authorities require certified translations for cross-border litigation, property transactions and probate proceedings.

What Documents Can Be Translated?

Virtually any document can be notarially translated. The translation can be performed on different types of source material depending on your needs and the requirements of the receiving authority.

Notarial copies: In most cases, the notarial translation is performed on a notarized copy of the original document. This is the most common approach — the notary first makes a certified copy of your original, and the translator then translates this copy. Your original document remains safe and untouched.

Original documents: Some authorities require the translation to be attached directly to the original document. In this case, the translation is bound to the original with the notary's certification. We will advise you if this is required for your specific situation.

Scans and electronic copies: If you are located outside Russia and cannot send the physical document, we can arrange for the translation to be done on the basis of a scan or photo. The final notarial certification will be performed when our office has access to the document. Contact us to discuss the options available for your case.

The most common questions about certified notarial translation of documents.

What is the difference between notarial and regular translation? ▼
A regular translation is simply a linguistic conversion of the document text. A notarial (notary-certified) translation is a translation performed by a professional translator whose signature is then certified by a notary public. The notary confirms the translator's identity and qualifications, which gives the translation legal weight. Notarial translations are accepted by most authorities worldwide, including immigration offices, courts and universities.

Can a copy be translated instead of the original? ▼
Yes. In many cases, the notarial translation is performed on a notarized copy of the original document rather than on the original itself. This means you can keep your original document safe. We can arrange for a notarized copy to be made and then have it translated with notarial certification.
How long does notarial translation take? ▼

Is a notarially certified translation accepted abroad? ▼
Notarially certified translations are accepted by the majority of authorities worldwide. We know the requirements for each country and will recommend the correct type of certified translation for your destination.
What is a sworn translator? ▼
A sworn translator (also called a certified or authorized translator) is a language professional who has been officially appointed and sworn in by a government authority or court. In Germany, this is a Vereidigter Übersetzer; in Spain, a traductor jurado; in France, a traducteur assermenté. A sworn translator's signature and stamp are recognized directly by authorities without additional notarial certification. Some countries accept only sworn translator translations and not notary-certified ones.
