How to Get Documents from Russia to the USA

The United States has one of the largest Russian-speaking communities in the world — over 3 million people. Whether you are applying for a US visa, green card, citizenship, marriage registration, university admission or professional licensing, US authorities will require official documents issued in Russia. These documents must be properly legalized and translated before they can be accepted by USCIS, NVC, embassies, courts, universities or employers.

The process of preparing Russian documents for use in the United States involves several critical steps: obtaining the original document from the Russian registry or authority, getting an apostille (since both Russia and the US are members of the 1961 Hague Convention), translating the document into English by a certified translator, and in some cases obtaining additional certifications from the Secretary of State or the US Embassy. Navigating these requirements can be complex and time-consuming, especially if you are already living in the United States and cannot travel to Russia. Apostille for US Immigration and Authorities

When you submit Russian documents to US authorities — whether it is USCIS, NVC, US Embassy in Moscow, a US court, a university or an employer — the documents must bear an apostille to be considered authentic. An apostille is a standardized certification issued under the 1961 Hague Convention that verifies the authority of the official who signed the document and the authenticity of their seal.

In Russia, apostilles are issued by different authorities depending on the document type. Civil registry documents are apostilled by the regional ZAGS department or the regional Ministry of Justice. Educational documents receive apostilles from the regional Department of Education. Court decisions and notarized documents are apostilled by the regional court. Our team has years of experience navigating these bureaucratic channels, and we know exactly which authority to approach for each specific document.

The processing time for an apostille depends on the type of document, the issuing region and the competent authority. Contact us and we will provide an individual assessment of the expected processing time for your specific case. Every apostille we obtain is thoroughly verified for accuracy before shipping.

Certified Translation for USCIS and US Authorities

USCIS requires that all foreign-language documents submitted as part of an immigration application be accompanied by a full English translation. The translation must be certified — meaning the translator must include a signed certification statement confirming that the translation is accurate and complete. USCIS does not require the translator to be officially licensed, but the certification statement must follow a specific format.

For other US purposes — such as university admissions, professional licensing (WES evaluation), court proceedings and employment — more formal translation requirements may apply. We work with experienced Russian-English translator teams who understand the specific requirements of different US authorities. Our translations are formatted to meet USCIS guidelines and include all required certification elements.

⚡ USCIS Tip: Never submit original documents to USCIS unless specifically requested. Always submit photocopies of the original documents along with the original apostille page and the certified translation. Keep your originals in a safe place.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about getting Russian documents for the United States.

Does USCIS require an apostille on Russian documents?▼
USCIS does not explicitly require an apostille — they accept copies of foreign documents with certified English translations. However, having an apostille adds a layer of authenticity and may be required by other US authorities (NVC, US courts, state agencies, universities). We recommend getting an apostille on all documents.
Do I need a "certified" or "notarized" translation for USCIS?▼
USCIS requires a "certified translation," which means the translator must include a signed statement certifying that the translation is accurate and complete. USCIS does not require the translator to be officially licensed or the translation to be notarized. However, some US courts and state agencies may require notarization. We provide both certified and notarized translations.
How long does it take to get documents from Russia for US immigration?▼
The processing time depends on the type of document, the region in Russia where it was issued, and the current workload of the competent authority. We always provide an individual estimate after reviewing your request. Contact us and we will assess the expected timeline for your specific case.
Can you help with the NVC document requirements?▼
Yes. When your case reaches the NVC stage, you will need various civil documents from Russia — birth certificates, marriage certificates, police clearance certificates, etc. We can obtain all of these documents, apostille them and provide certified English translations that meet NVC requirements.
Do I need to submit original documents or copies to USCIS?▼
In most cases, USCIS accepts photocopies of foreign documents along with certified translations. Original documents are generally only required at the interview stage. We recommend keeping your original apostilled documents in a safe place and submitting high-quality copies to USCIS.
